Trash-E : The Trash Sorting Robot

Intro to Mechatronics - Final Project



What's the project all about?

The Trash Sorting Robot was my final project for my MTE 100 (Introduction to Mechatronics) course in the University of Waterloo. The assignment was essentially to create a robot of our choice with a couple of requirements such as using multiple sensors and having at least 6 functions. However, we were allowed to use any materials we wanted to create the robot as long as it was safe. After a week of planning, my team of 4 decided to create a robot that will pick up a piece of trash and sort it into its respective area, which were colour coded. We used T - shaped blocks to represent our trash, green for compost, brown for garbage, blue for recycling, as well as red for the starting zone and black for the pick-up zone.


What tools did I use to make it?

We created Trashy using an EV3 kit we were given, along with aluminium extrusions, wheels, extra sensors, strings, and a lot of duct tape to secure everything together. In terms of coding, we used RobotC, and each person had different functions assigned to them.



What did I learn?

I learnt how the manufacturing process and coding came together to create a robot that can be moved without the need of a joystick or human interference. My coding skills had greatly improved as I spent a lot of time debugging and changing my code to make it more efficient. I also had to adapt to problems that arose, such as when the colour sensors did not pick up on certain colours. This meant we had to change both the physical design and code to counter the problem and make it work. We made the colour sensors closer to the ground, and made sure that the code could only pick up one colour and not switch.


What was my contribution?

I helped in the building of the robot, specifically making the chassis. I used a reference online and changed it to suit the materials we were given. It did prove to be a challenge at first since none of the parts would align or stay properly, but with a bit of experimentation and trying different combinations of designs, we got it to work. As for the coding side, I coded our follow line, colour path, and finish function. Essentially, the follow line function would see the colours on either side of the robot, and if one of them detected a colour it would move in the opposite way to correct its path. For the colour path function, it would detect the colour of the block and then follow a line until one of the colour sensors near the ground detected that specific colour, and if it did it would turn and stop once both colour sensors detect the ground. The finish function took Trashy to the start zone and made it “dance” (rotate a lot) and play a sound.



What are its implications?

If the robot were to be on a larger scale and can sort multiple variations and sizes of objects, then it can be a useful application in the real world. For example, there are many types of garbage or chemicals that can be dangerous if humans were exposed to it, in which case this robot would come in handy. A robot would also be able to sort trash for longer periods of time than humans and more efficiently. There could also be multiple robots to detect specific types of trash (ie. non-metal vs metal using reflectivity) using different types of sensors.
